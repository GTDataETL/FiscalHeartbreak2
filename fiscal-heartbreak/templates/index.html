Summary - Fiscal Heartbreak:

This project seeks to analyze the impact of US Fiscal Policy on US Family Households. More specifically, we examine the trends of Consumer Debt by county as compared to the trends of Divorce Rates by county.

Preliminary Hypothesis: Higher levels of consumer debt (measured by debt-to-income ratio or “DTI”) lead to higher rates of divorce and lower rates of marriage.

Null Hypothesis: There is no relationship between consumer debt and divorce rates.

In order to perform this analysis, we sourced marriage statistics by county for each of 3 years from the US Census ACS data (table S1201). Reference: Census Data Link In addition, we downloaded data of Household Debt compared to Household Income in the form of a Debt to Income Ratio (DTI) from Federal Reserve data. Reference: Federal Reserve Data Link

The following user acceptance tests were performed on the acquired data to ensure acceptable quality:

  • Debt-to-Income Ratio Boundaries: ensured that no lower debt-to-income ratio bands exceeded upper debt-to-income ratio band in any given row in order to confirm data reliability and accuracy. This was confirmed by visualizing our debt-to-income ratio differential (upper-band minus lower-band) in ascending order.

  • Sum of Marriage Statistics: Made sure that the sum of overall marriage statistic percentages tied out to ~100% to ensure that data found in each record was complete and accurate. The extrema of the total percentages calculated ranged from 99.8% to 100.2%, which was identified as being caused by insignificant rounding adjustments within the original datasets.

Once the data was cleansed and loaded, we generated an API and developed a web application to consume and visualize the data in a user-friendly manner. Integrating a map, scatter plot, bar chart, and regression analysis onto a single page with linked user interactions allowed us to identify a positive trend and reject the null hypothesis.
